ingredients:
  - 2 (15-ounce) cans chickpeas, drained and rinsed
  - 1 medium yellow onion, chopped
  - 6 cloves garlic, chopped
  - 4 tbsp chopped fresh parsley
  - 4 tsp ground coriander
  - 1 tbsp arrowroot
  - 2 tsp ground cumin
  - Sea salt and black pepper, to taste
instructions:
  - Preheat the oven to 400°F. Line a baking sheet with parchment paper.
  - Combine all ingredients (chickpeas, onion, garlic, parsley, arrowroot, coriander, cumin, and salt and pepper to taste) in a food processor and process, leaving a little texture to the beans.
  - Shape the mixture into balls, using a small ice cream scoop or tablespoon. Place them on the baking sheet, and bake for 25 minutes.
  - Turn the falafel balls over, and bake for 20 minutes more.
notes:
  - Leave a little texture to the beans when processing - don't make it completely smooth.
  - Good as an appetizer or in a pita with green sauce.
  - Total baking time is 45 minutes (25 minutes + 20 minutes after turning).

## The Art of Perfect Falafel

Falafel is one of the Middle East's greatest culinary gifts to the world – crispy, herb-packed fritters that have been enjoyed throughout the Levant region for centuries. While their exact origin is debated (with Egyptians, Palestinians, and Lebanese all claiming credit), what's undeniable is the satisfaction these humble chickpea fritters provide, whether served in a pita pocket, atop a salad, or as part of a mezze spread.

What distinguishes truly exceptional falafel from mediocre versions is the texture – a shattering crisp exterior that gives way to a light, fluffy, herb-flecked interior that's never dense or pasty. This perfect texture is achieved through several key techniques: using dried chickpeas rather than canned, incorporating ample fresh herbs, and maintaining the proper balance of moisture in the mixture.

The vibrant green interior of authentic falafel comes from the generous use of fresh herbs. Parsley and cilantro provide the classic color and flavor, while mint adds a subtle brightness that elevates the entire dish. Combined with aromatic spices like cumin and coriander, these herbs transform simple chickpeas into something truly craveable.

## The Critical Chickpea Choice

The foundation of great falafel is properly prepared chickpeas, and this is where many home recipes go wrong. Here's why dried chickpeas are non-negotiable:

- **Texture control**: Dried chickpeas, soaked but uncooked, provide the perfect consistency that creates light, fluffy falafel
- **Binding properties**: Raw soaked chickpeas contain starches that help the mixture hold together without excessive flour
- **Moisture level**: Canned chickpeas contain too much moisture, resulting in falafels that fall apart during frying
- **Flavor profile**: Uncooked chickpeas have a cleaner, more pronounced flavor than their pre-cooked counterparts

The overnight soak is essential, allowing the chickpeas to soften enough for grinding while still maintaining their structural integrity. This seemingly simple step is the secret to falafel that holds together during frying without becoming heavy or dense.

## Serving Traditions

Falafel can be enjoyed in numerous ways, each with regional variations:

- **Sandwich style**: Stuffed in pita with tahini sauce, Israeli salad (diced tomatoes and cucumbers), pickled vegetables, and sometimes French fries
- **Mezze platter**: Served as part of an assortment of small dishes alongside hummus, baba ganoush, tabbouleh, and warm bread
- **Salad topper**: Placed atop a bed of greens with tomatoes, cucumbers, and a lemon-tahini dressing
- **Breakfast option**: In some parts of the Middle East, falafel is enjoyed as a morning meal with eggs and bread

The tahini sauce is the classic accompaniment, its creamy richness providing the perfect counterpoint to the crispy fritters. The simple combination of tahini paste, lemon juice, garlic, and water creates a sauce that's both humble and sophisticated – the ideal partner to falafel's earthy flavors.

## Regional Variations

Falafel varies across different Middle Eastern regions:

- **Egyptian style**: Made with fava beans (called ta'ameya) instead of chickpeas, with more herbs and spices
- **Lebanese version**: Typically smaller in size with a higher herb content and often served with turnip pickles
- **Israeli adaptation**: Usually larger, served in a full pita pocket with extensive toppings and sauces
- **Jordanian approach**: Often incorporates more onion and a touch of baking soda for extra fluffiness

## Variations

### Fava Bean Falafel (Ta'ameya)
Replace half or all of the chickpeas with dried fava beans (soaked overnight) for an Egyptian-style falafel with a creamier texture.

### Spicy Falafel
Add 1-2 jalapeños or serrano peppers to the food processor with the herbs for a spicier kick, perfect for heat lovers.

### Red Pepper Falafel
Add 1/2 cup roasted red peppers to the mixture for a slightly sweeter, more complex flavor profile and beautiful color contrast.

### Sesame Falafel
Roll the formed falafel balls in sesame seeds before frying for an extra layer of crunch and nutty flavor that complements the tahini sauce.
